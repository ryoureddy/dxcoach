# Illness Script: Pulmonary Embolism

## Pathophysiology
- Thrombus (most commonly from proximal DVT) lodges in pulmonary arteries → abrupt increase in RV afterload → RV dilation and failure → decreased cardiac output
- V/Q mismatch in obstructed vascular beds → hypoxemia; loss of surfactant distal to obstruction can cause pulmonary infarction (hemoptysis, pleuritic pain)
- Massive PE → obstructive shock; chronic unresolved clot → chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension (CTEPH)

## Epidemiology
- Prior DVT/PE is the single strongest risk factor — ~30% recurrence risk without anticoagulation
- Major provoking factors: recent surgery (esp. orthopedic), prolonged immobility, active malignancy (highest risk: pancreatic, lung, GI cancers)
- Moderate provoking factors: obesity, pregnancy/postpartum, oral contraceptives/hormone replacement, long-haul travel
- Thrombophilia: Factor V Leiden, antiphospholipid syndrome, protein C/S deficiency
- Incidental/unprovoked PE: ~30% of cases — warrants workup for occult malignancy and thrombophilia

## Time Course
Acute onset over minutes to hours; untreated massive PE can progress to RV failure and cardiac arrest within hours. Submassive PE may stabilize but carries risk of hemodynamic deterioration within the first 48–72 hours.

## Salient Symptoms & Signs
- Dyspnea — acute onset, often at rest or out of proportion to physical findings (Sx) — most common presenting symptom (~80%)
- Pleuritic chest pain (Sx) — lateral, worsens with inspiration; indicates peripheral PE with pulmonary infarction
- Hemoptysis (Sx) — small-volume, bright red; classic triad with pleuritic pain and DVT symptoms
- Tachycardia (Sn) — most common objective finding; persistent despite supplemental O₂
- Hypoxia (Sn) — often disproportionate to CXR findings; may be normal at rest and worsen with exertion
- Unilateral leg swelling/erythema (Sn) — DVT present in ~50% of PE cases; absence does not exclude PE
- Hypotension or syncope (Sx/Sn) — indicates massive (high-risk) PE with hemodynamic compromise

## Diagnostics
- CTPA (CT pulmonary angiography) — gold standard; shows intraluminal filling defects; simultaneously assesses RV/LV ratio (>0.9 indicates RV strain)
- D-dimer — high sensitivity (~97%), low specificity; use age-adjusted cutoff (age × 10 ng/mL for patients >50); negative result effectively rules out PE in low-to-intermediate probability patients
- Wells Score — stratifies pre-test probability (≤4 = PE unlikely; >4 = PE likely); guides D-dimer vs. direct CTPA pathway
- ECG — sinus tachycardia (most common); S1Q3T3 pattern in ~20% (specific but insensitive); RBBB or RV strain pattern suggests massive PE
- Troponin + BNP/NT-proBNP — elevated in RV myocardial injury; combined elevation (troponin positive + RV dilation on echo/CT) defines intermediate-high-risk PE
- Echo — bedside assessment of RV function; McConnell's sign (RV free wall hypokinesis with spared apex) is specific for massive PE; useful when CTPA cannot be performed

## Treatment
- Anticoagulation is the cornerstone for all risk categories: DOACs (rivaroxaban, apixaban) preferred over VKA for most patients — faster onset, fewer drug interactions, no routine monitoring required
- Low-risk PE: anticoagulation alone; selected patients can be managed safely as outpatients (Hestia criteria)
- Intermediate-high-risk PE (RV dysfunction + troponin positive, hemodynamically stable): anticoagulation with close monitoring; catheter-directed thrombolysis (CDT) is an option over systemic thrombolysis given lower bleeding risk; mechanical thrombectomy emerging as alternative
- High-risk (massive) PE (hemodynamic instability): systemic thrombolysis (alteplase) if no contraindication; surgical embolectomy or percutaneous mechanical thrombectomy if thrombolysis fails or is contraindicated
- Duration: 3 months if provoked by transient risk factor; indefinite if unprovoked, cancer-associated, or recurrent VTE

## Key Discriminators
- Sudden dyspnea + hypoxia disproportionate to CXR findings → favors PE over pneumonia or CHF (where CXR is usually abnormal)
- Pleuritic chest pain + hemoptysis + unilateral leg swelling → virtually pathognomonic for PE over ACS or pneumothorax
- RV dilation + troponin elevation without LV disease or coronary lesion → favors massive PE over NSTEMI
- S1Q3T3 or new RBBB on ECG in the setting of acute dyspnea → favors PE over primary arrhythmia or cardiac ischemia
- Normal or near-normal CXR in hypoxic, tachycardic patient → strongly favors PE over pneumonia or pulmonary edema

## Disease Mimickers
- **Pneumonia:** dyspnea + pleuritic pain overlap, but PE lacks productive cough, fever, and lobar consolidation; CXR is normal or shows only a peripheral wedge opacity (Hampton's hump)
- **ACS (NSTEMI/UA):** both cause chest pain and troponin elevation, but PE produces RV pattern on echo/ECG rather than LV ischemia, and coronary angiogram is normal
- **COPD exacerbation:** acute dyspnea + hypoxia in both; PE lacks wheezing, hypercapnia, and infective trigger — and PE is a recognized precipitant of apparent AECOPD (~13% of cases)
