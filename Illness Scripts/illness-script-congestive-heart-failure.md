# Illness Script: Congestive Heart Failure

## Pathophysiology
- **HFrEF (EF <40%):** Cardiomyocyte death or dysfunction (MI, cardiomyopathy, myocarditis) → LV dilation + neurohormonal activation (RAAS, sympathetic) → adverse remodeling → progressive systolic failure and reduced cardiac output
- **HFpEF (EF ≥50%):** HTN, obesity, DM, and metabolic inflammation → LV stiffening and diastolic dysfunction → elevated filling pressures despite preserved EF; driven by microvascular dysfunction, myocardial fibrosis, and impaired NO-cGMP signaling
- Both phenotypes: sodium/water retention + venous congestion → pulmonary and peripheral edema; neurohormonal activation perpetuates the cycle

## Epidemiology
- **HFrEF risk factors:** prior MI (strongest — accounts for ~60% of HFrEF), dilated cardiomyopathy (idiopathic, viral, alcohol, anthracyclines), uncontrolled hypertension, peripartum
- **HFpEF risk factors:** obesity, hypertension, diabetes, older age (>65), female sex, atrial fibrillation, chronic kidney disease — HFpEF now accounts for ~50–65% of all HF cases
- Prevalence ~2% general population; rises to >10% in adults over 70 years
- 5-year mortality ~50%, similar across HFrEF and HFpEF; leading cause of hospitalization in adults >65

## Time Course
Chronic, progressive syndrome punctuated by acute decompensations (ADHF); stable HF worsens over months to years as remodeling progresses. ADHF typically evolves over days with rapid volume accumulation; once decompensated, untreated acute CHF can progress to flash pulmonary edema or cardiogenic shock.

## Salient Symptoms & Signs
- Dyspnea on exertion progressing to dyspnea at rest — the cardinal symptom (Sx); severity follows NYHA Class I–IV
- Orthopnea and paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea (PND) (Sx) — highly specific for elevated LV filling pressure
- Bilateral pitting leg edema (Sn) — dependent, symmetric; absent in acute flash pulmonary edema
- Elevated JVP (Sn) — most reliable sign of elevated right heart pressures; hepatojugular reflux increases sensitivity
- S3 gallop (Sn) — highly specific for volume overload/systolic dysfunction; indicates elevated LVEDP
- Bibasilar crackles (Sn) — may be absent in chronic compensated HF despite elevated pressures
- Displaced point of maximal impulse (PMI) (Sn) — lateral displacement indicates LV dilation; distinguishes HFrEF from HFpEF at bedside

## Diagnostics
- **Echo** — definitive test; classifies HFrEF (EF <40%) vs. HFmrEF (40–49%) vs. HFpEF (≥50%); assesses diastolic dysfunction (E/e' ratio, LAVI, LV mass) and valve disease; required for all new HF diagnoses
- **BNP/NT-proBNP** — elevated with increased filling pressures; NT-proBNP >125 pg/mL or BNP >35 pg/mL supports HF diagnosis; falsely low in obesity (~50% lower thresholds); rising levels signal decompensation and guide prognosis
- **CXR** — cardiomegaly, pulmonary vascular cephalization, Kerley B lines, interstitial/alveolar edema, pleural effusions (bilateral > unilateral); normal CXR does not exclude HFpEF
- **ECG** — identifies LBBB (indicates CRT candidacy if EF ≤35%), LVH (HFpEF phenotype), AF (common precipitant/comorbidity), prior MI (Q waves in HFrEF)
- **BMP** — renal function (CRS), sodium (hyponatremia = poor prognosis), potassium (guides diuretic and RAAS dosing)

## Treatment
- **HFrEF — Quadruple GDMT (all four classes reduce mortality):** ARNI (sacubitril-valsartan, preferred over ACEi/ARB) + beta-blocker (carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, bisoprolol) + MRA (spironolactone/eplerenone) + SGLT2i (empagliflozin/dapagliflozin); simultaneous/rapid initiation is preferred over sequential up-titration
- **Device therapy (EF ≤35%):** ICD for sudden cardiac death prevention; CRT if LBBB with QRS ≥150 ms — improves EF, symptoms, and mortality
- **HFpEF:** SGLT2i reduces HF hospitalization (EMPEROR-Preserved, DELIVER trials); loop diuretics for decongestion; aggressive management of HTN, DM, and obesity (GLP-1 agonists emerging for obese HFpEF); non-steroidal MRA (finerenone) being studied
- **Acute decompensated HF:** IV loop diuretics (furosemide) first-line for decongestion; vasodilators (nitroglycerin, nitroprusside) for hypertensive ADHF; inotropes/vasopressors only for cardiogenic shock

## Key Discriminators
- Orthopnea + PND + elevated JVP + S3 → favors CHF over COPD exacerbation or PE (all of which can cause acute dyspnea)
- Cardiomegaly + bilateral pleural effusions + pulmonary cephalization on CXR → favors CHF over pneumonia or PE (which show focal opacity or clear lung fields respectively)
- Markedly elevated BNP + bilateral symmetric leg edema + dyspnea relieved by sitting upright → favors CHF over hepatic cirrhosis (JVP is normal in cirrhosis, BNP is not significantly elevated)
- LBBB or AF + elevated BNP + reduced LVEF on echo → confirms HFrEF over HFpEF mimickers (hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, cardiac tamponade, pericardial constriction)
- BNP falsely low in obese patients — use weight-adjusted or lower thresholds; do not rule out HFpEF based on BNP alone in obese patients

## Disease Mimickers
- **COPD exacerbation:** both cause acute dyspnea and hypoxia, but COPD shows hyperinflation/flattened diaphragms on CXR, normal BNP, and responds to bronchodilators rather than diuretics
- **Hepatic cirrhosis:** bilateral leg edema and ascites overlap, but cirrhosis has normal JVP, absence of pulmonary congestion, and elevated liver enzymes/low albumin
- **Pulmonary embolism:** acute dyspnea + elevated BNP can mimic ADHF, but PE features RV strain on echo/ECG, clear or near-normal CXR, and no signs of left-sided volume overload
